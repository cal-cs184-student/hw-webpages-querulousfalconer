Overview (3 pts)

This project implements a Monte Carlo path tracer: primary rays are cast from a pinhole camera through each pixel; intersections against scene geometry (triangles and spheres) are accelerated with a bounding volume hierarchy (BVH). Shading combines emitted light (zero-bounce), direct illumination at the first surface (either uniform hemisphere sampling or importance sampling of area lights), and indirect light from recursive BSDF sampling with Russian roulette termination. Adaptive sampling stops adding pixel samples once a confidence interval on mean brightness falls below a tolerance. Together, these pieces turn a ray caster into a physically motivated renderer that captures soft shadows, color bleeding, and depth of field (when enabled), at the cost of noise that high sample counts and light sampling reduce.

The most interesting takeaway for me was seeing how where you sample (hemisphere vs lights, BSDF vs uniform) dominates both noise and runtime more than small algorithmic tweaks elsewhere. The BVH made large scenes tractable, and adaptive sampling showed that not every pixel needs the same workload.


Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection (20 pts)

Ray generation. For each pixel sample, the tracer jitters a 2D point inside the pixel and maps it to normalized coordinates $(u,v) \in [0,1]^2$ with the image origin at the bottom-left. Camera::generate_ray places the sensor on the canonical plane at $z=-1$, sized from the horizontal and vertical fields of view, builds a direction in camera space, transforms it to world space with the camera-to-world matrix c2w, and emits a ray from the camera position with near/far clip distances nClip and fClip.

Intersection. The ray is tested against the scene via the BVH. Leaf nodes perform primitive tests: spheres use analytic ray–sphere intersection; triangles use the Möller–Trumbore test. The closest hit updates an Intersection record (distance, interpolated normal, BSDF pointer) used for shading.


We use the Möller–Trumbore formulation. Let $\mathbf{e}_1 = \mathbf{p}_2-\mathbf{p}_1$ and $\mathbf{e}_2 = \mathbf{p}_3-\mathbf{p}_1$ span the triangle plane. The ray is $\mathbf{r}(t)=\mathbf{o}+t\mathbf{d}$. Cross products reuse work so we solve for barycentric coordinates $u,v$ and distance $t$ with few dot products. We reject hits if the ray is parallel to the plane, if $(u,v)$ fall outside the triangle ($u<0$, $v<0$, $u+v>1$), or if $t$ is outside the ray’s valid $t$ range. For shading, $w=1-u-v$ and the geometric normal is interpolated as $w\mathbf{n}_1+u\mathbf{n}_2+v\mathbf{n}_3$ for smooth meshes.

Part 2: Bounding Volume Hierarchy (17 pts)

Walk through your BVH construction algorithm. Explain the heuristic you chose for picking the splitting point.

BVHAccel::construct_bvh recursively partitions a range of primitives. We first compute an axis-aligned bounding box of all primitives in the range. If the count is at most max_leaf_size, we store a leaf referencing that index range. Otherwise we pick the split axis as the one with the largest extent of that bounding box, sort primitives by the centroid coordinate along that axis, then choose a split index that minimizes a surface-area style cost: for each candidate split between sorted neighbors, we combine the surface areas of the left and right child bounding boxes (prefix/suffix boxes) weighted by the number of primitives on each side, and take the minimum. That is a standard SAH-inspired partition without an explicit intersection cost term. If the best split is degenerate, we fall back to a leaf.

Traversal. Stack-based traversal tests the ray against each node’s BBox; leaves test contained primitives and track the closest hit.

Compare rendering times with and without BVH acceleration (one paragraph).

Without a BVH, each ray may test every primitive, so cost scales roughly with rays × triangles. With a BVH, average tests per ray drop sharply on large meshes, so wall-clock time falls dramatically while images match; the gap widens as triangle count grows. I tested this on the Lucy and dragon scenes, and saw modest speedups on tiny scenes and very large speedups on Lucy or dragon-scale assets. I also found there was an 20-40% speedup on the building scene, which is a smaller scene but still has a lot of triangles.


Part 3: Direct Illumination (20 pts)

Walk through both implementations of the direct lighting function.

Uniform hemisphere (estimate_direct_lighting_hemisphere). At the hit point we build an orthonormal frame from the shading normal. We draw directions uniformly over the upper hemisphere in local space, transform to world space, and cast shadow rays. If we hit an emissive BSDF, we accumulate $L_i \, f_r \, \cos\theta_i$ divided by the hemisphere pdf $1/(2\pi)$, averaged over ns_area_light samples (the starter uses this count for hemisphere samples as well).

Importance sampling lights (estimate_direct_lighting_importance). For each scene light we call sample_L, which returns an incident direction, distance, and pdf. We trace a shadow ray up to just before the light. If unoccluded, we add $L_i \, f_r \, \cos\theta_i / \mathrm{pdf}$, averaging over multiple samples for area lights and a single sample for delta lights.

The integrator selects between them via direct_hemisphere_sample (GUI) or -H in headless mode for hemisphere sampling; otherwise light sampling is used.

Uniform hemisphere vs light sampling (one paragraph).

Hemisphere sampling probes the whole upper hemisphere with equal probability, so finding a small bright emitter is a rare event; variance is high and shadows and caustic-like direct paths are noisy at equal sample counts. Sampling the light source explicitly generates directions that land on the emitter with probability tied to its solid angle from the shading point, which matches the importance of direct lighting; shadow boundaries converge much faster. Hemisphere sampling remains a useful baseline and is closer to a naive path tracer, but for scenes dominated by a few lights, importance sampling those lights is the practical choice.


Part 4: Global Illumination (25 pts)

Walk through your implementation of the indirect lighting function.

at_least_one_bounce_radiance adds direct lighting at the current vertex according to the accumulate flag (see below), then if depth allows, samples the BSDF with sample_f to get an incoming direction, pdf, and $f_r$. Russian roulette: except when we need at least one extension, with probability $0.35$ we terminate; otherwise we scale surviving paths by $1/(1-0.35)$. We trace the continuation ray and add $f_r \, L_\mathrm{next} \, \cos\theta_i / (\mathrm{pdf} \cdot p_\mathrm{continue})$. Emission at the camera’s first hit is added separately as zero-bounce radiance in est_radiance_global_illumination.

What to discuss for the 2nd and 3rd bounce: the second bounce typically begins to show light that has reflected once already (e.g. ground tint on the bunny, softer fill); the third bounce adds another interreflection pass, often visible in concavities and contact regions. Rasterization with only local shading does not capture these multi-bounce paths unless you add bespoke hacks (e.g. baked radiosity); path tracing unifies them in one integrator.

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling (15 pts)

Explain adaptive sampling and your implementation.

Adaptive sampling spends more work where the pixel mean is still uncertain. In raytrace_pixel, samples are taken in batches. After each batch we estimate the mean illuminance and its variance, form an approximate 95% confidence interval half-width $I = 1.96\,\sigma/\sqrt{n}$, and stop early if $I \le \texttt{maxTolerance} \times \text{mean}$ (and $n>1$). The final per-pixel sample count is stored in sampleCountBuffer for visualization. Parameters samplesPerBatch and maxTolerance come from the app config (-a in headless mode).

Below: motion blur, a k-d tree accelerator compared to the BVH, and bilateral denoising on the HDR buffer before tonemapping. Command-line flags and a small reproducible script are in the repository; copy outputs from build/ into docs/images/ (or re-render) to match the paths used here.

Motion blur

Each ray carries a shutter time time $\in [0,1]$ (src/pathtracer/ray.h). For meshes, vertex positions at the end of the shutter interval live in Mesh::positions_end (same length as positions); triangles interpolate linearly between positions[i] and positions_end[i] inside Möller–Trumbore (src/scene/triangle.cpp). Bounding boxes enclose both poses so acceleration remains conservative. Spheres support linear motion of the center via SphereObject::set_motion_end_center. Secondary rays (direct shadow and indirect bounces) reuse the primary ray’s time so the shutter is frozen along a path. Enable per-pixel random times with -M (see src/application/main.cpp).

Demo: if the environment variable CGL_PROOF_MOTION is set when the scene loads, the renderer adds a fixed translation to the first mesh’s end positions (see apply_proof_motion_scene in src/pathtracer/raytraced_renderer.cpp) so -M produces visible streaking. Reproduce with ./scripts/run_feature_proof.sh or: CGL_PROOF_MOTION=1 ./build/pathtracer -M -s 64 -m 2 -f docs/images/part6_motion_blur.png -r 256 256 dae/simple/cube.dae and a static baseline without -M / without the env var for comparison.


Alternative acceleration: k-d tree vs BVH

We added KDTreeAccel (src/scene/kdtree_accel.cpp): median splits on cycling axes over primitive centroids; leaves store index ranges; traversal is recursive with axis-aligned node bounds (disjoint half-spaces, unlike overlapping BVH siblings). BVHAccel and KDTreeAccel share a RayAccelerator base (src/scene/ray_accel.h) for ray and intersection-test counters. Choose the structure with -A bvh (default) or -A kd. BVH node visualization in the GUI still requires a BVH; with a k-d tree, that mode is disabled.

Measurement: -B runs a micro-benchmark after loading the scene: build time for both structures (on copies of the primitive list), then trace the same $250{,}000$ random rays inside the scene bounding box. The console reports trace time, Mrays/s, and average primitive intersection tests per ray. Example output (machine-dependent; re-run locally):

Bilateral denoising

After accumulating HDR linear radiance in sampleBuffer, HDRImageBuffer::bilateral_denoise (src/util/image.h) applies a spatial Gaussian kernel weighted by luminance similarity, then the usual gamma toColor path runs for display (PathTracer::write_to_framebuffer). Enable with -N <sigma_range>; spatial $\sigma$ and half-window use pathtracer defaults (configurable in code). Example: ./build/pathtracer -N 0.12 -s 4 -m 2 -f docs/images/part6_bilateral.png -r 256 256 dae/meshedit/cow.dae. Compare to the same settings without -N for part6_bilateral_baseline.png.

Bilateral filtering reduces high-frequency noise but can smooth fine detail and bright edges; it is a cheap post-pass, not a full Monte Carlo denoiser (OIDN, etc.).
