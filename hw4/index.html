Overview

In this assignment, I implemented a full cloth simulation and shading pipeline. On the simulation side, I built a mass-spring cloth model with structural, shearing, and bending springs; accumulated external and spring forces; advanced point masses with Verlet integration; enforced Provot's 10% stretch limit; and added collision handling for planes, spheres, and self-collision using spatial hashing.

On the rendering side, I implemented and tested multiple shaders: Blinn-Phong, texture mapping, bump mapping, displacement mapping, and mirror environment mapping, and I wired these into the simulator's shader picker. As extra simulation features, I also integrated spatially varying wind and keyboard-driven collision-object motion for interactive experiments.

The most interesting takeaway for me was how strongly behavior depends on physically meaningful parameters. Small changes in stiffness, damping, and density can produce dramatically different motion, and the shading work reinforced the difference between changing appearance (normal perturbation) and changing geometry (true vertex displacement).

Part 1: Masses and springs

I first created a grid of point masses over cloth width/height and set their initial positions based on orientation. For horizontal cloth, masses are placed on the \(x\)-\(z\) plane at \(y=1\); for vertical cloth, masses are placed on the \(x\)-\(y\) plane with a small random \(z\)-offset.

Then, for each point mass, I connected structural springs to the left and up neighbors, shearing springs to up-left and up-right neighbors, and bending springs to masses two steps left/up. This constructs the expected cloth connectivity while avoiding duplicate spring creation.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

Each time step computes net force on every point mass as: external forces (gravity, plus optional wind) and internal spring forces from Hooke's law. For spring force, I used \(F = k_s (|x|-l_0)\hat{x}\), and for bending springs I scaled stiffness by 0.2.

I integrated motion with Verlet: \[ p_{t+\Delta t} = p_t + (1-\text{damping})(p_t - p_{t-\Delta t}) + a_t \Delta t^2 \] for non-pinned masses. After integration and collisions, I applied Provot correction so any spring stretch above 10% of rest length is projected back, splitting correction evenly unless an endpoint is pinned.

I paused with P, changed parameters in the GUI, resumed with P, and reset with R for controlled comparisons.

Effect of ks (spring stiffness)

Very low \(k_s\) makes the cloth floppy and stretchy: it sags more, folds deeply, and takes longer to settle. Very high \(k_s\) keeps the cloth taut with smaller local deformation and less stretch under the same gravity/collision conditions.

Effect of density

Since point mass is proportional to density, increasing density increases inertia. Heavier cloth accelerates less from the same spring force, tends to pull downward more strongly in draping setups, and usually settles into broader, heavier-looking folds. Lower density responds faster and appears lighter.

Effect of damping

Low damping preserves velocity between steps, so oscillations persist and the cloth bounces for longer. High damping removes kinetic energy quickly, producing faster convergence to rest with less overshoot.

Parameters used for this final render: I used \(k_s=5000\), density \(=150\), damping \(=0.2\).

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

Sphere collisions: I tested whether a point mass enters the sphere by checking if distance to sphere center is less than radius. For collisions, I compute the tangent point on the sphere surface in the radial direction and move the mass to a friction-adjusted corrected position using: pm.position = pm.last_position + (1 - friction) * correction.

Plane collisions: I detected crossing by checking the sign change of the signed distance to the plane between last and current positions. If crossing occurs, I solve for the segment-plane intersection parameter \(t\), compute the tangent hit point, then offset slightly along the normal to avoid sticking/precision issues and apply friction-scaled correction from last_position.

Qualitatively, low \(k_s\) drapes and stretches more around the sphere; high \(k_s\) keeps shape more rigid and produces tighter, less stretched contact regions.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

I implemented self-collision using spatial hashing. Each frame, I bucket point masses by hashed 3D cell coordinates (cell sizes based on cloth dimensions). For each point mass, I only test nearby candidates in its own bucket.

If another mass is closer than \(2 \cdot \text{thickness}\), I accumulate a correction vector that pushes them apart. After processing candidates, I apply the average correction scaled by 1 / simulation_steps to keep the response stable across substeps.

This prevents cloth points from collapsing into each other and allows realistic folding and stacking during free fall and ground contact.

Parameter study: increasing density generally increases inertia and produces heavier-looking folds; increasing \(k_s\) reduces stretch and gives sharper folds; decreasing \(k_s\) allows more pronounced sagging and slower settling.

Part 5: Shaders

A shader program is GPU code that runs per vertex and per fragment. The vertex shader transforms geometry and passes interpolated attributes (position, normal, tangent, UV) to the fragment shader. The fragment shader computes final color from lighting, textures, and material logic. Together they define both the look and apparent detail of surfaces in real time.

Blinn-Phong shading model

In my implementation, the final color is ambient + diffuse + specular with inverse-square light falloff. Ambient gives a base term, diffuse uses \(\max(0,\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{l})\), and specular uses \(\max(0,\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{h})^p\), where \(\mathbf{h}\) is the normalized half-vector between light and view directions.

Texture mapping

Texture mapping samples from u_texture_1 with interpolated UVs and outputs that sampled color directly.

Bump mapping and displacement mapping

Bump mapping perturbs normals in the fragment shader using texture height derivatives and TBN basis, so it changes lighting response without changing mesh silhouette. Displacement mapping offsets vertex positions in the vertex shader by normal direction scaled by texture height, so geometry itself changes. For both effects, I use HMPDFT-ChibiMik.png as the shared height texture.

With coarse tessellation, displacement has limited geometric detail because there are fewer vertices to move, while bump still shows shading detail. With fine tessellation, displacement produces much richer geometric relief and silhouette changes.

Mirror shader

The mirror shader computes reflection direction with reflect(-wo, n) and samples the cubemap using that direction.

Custom shader

My current custom shader visualizes normals directly by remapping normal components from \([-1,1]\) to \([0,1]\), giving a normal-debug coloration.

Extra Credit

Spatially varying wind

I added per-triangle aerodynamic wind force. For each triangle, I compute area and normal, project local wind onto the normal, and distribute force equally to the triangle's three masses. Wind magnitude is modulated by a sinusoid of centroid position and simulation time, creating traveling gust patterns instead of uniform forcing.

Interactive collision-object motion

I added keyboard controls to translate collision objects at runtime: arrow keys move in \(x/y\), and PageUp/PageDown move in \(z\), with adjustable step size. This makes collision experiments interactive and easier to document.
